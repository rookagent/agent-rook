# Pet Photography

## Why Pet Sessions Are Great Business
- Pet owners spend money on their animals. Average pet parent spends more on pet photos than you'd expect.
- High social media shareability — pet photos go viral. Free marketing.
- Lower pressure than weddings. Fun, casual, repeatable.
- Great add-on to family sessions ("bring the dog!").
- Pet minis sell out fast.

## Session Planning
- **Duration:** 30-45 minutes for a dedicated pet session. 10-15 min add-on for family sessions.
- **Location:** Parks, fields, the pet's home, studio with a backdrop. Fenced areas are safest for off-leash shots.
- **Time of day:** Depends on the animal. Dogs do well anytime. Avoid peak heat for brachycephalic breeds (bulldogs, pugs).
- **Ask in advance:** Breed, temperament, any fears or triggers (other dogs, loud noises), favorite treats, favorite toy, any commands they know.

## Working with Dogs
- **Treats are everything.** Hold a treat next to your lens for eye contact. Squeaky toys work too.
- **Let them sniff you first.** Don't rush into the session. 5 minutes of greeting = a calmer dog for the rest.
- **Burst mode.** Dogs change expression in milliseconds. Ears up, tongue out, head tilt — you need burst.
- **Shoot at their level.** Get on the ground. Eye-level shots are 10x more engaging than shooting down at them.
- **Action shots:** Running, jumping, catching treats mid-air. Use continuous AF, fast shutter (1/1000+), pre-focus on a spot.
- **Owner behind you:** Have the owner stand directly behind you calling the dog's name. Gets the "looking right at camera" shot.
- **Leash removal in post:** Shoot with leash for safety, remove in Photoshop. Or use a thin leash that's easy to clone out.

## Working with Cats
- **Go to them.** Cats don't do well in new environments. Shoot at their home or accept studio chaos.
- **Patience.** Cats do what they want. Follow them with the camera.
- **Window light.** Cats gravitate toward windows. Let them.
- **Toys on strings.** Feather wands, laser pointers (catch the look-up, not the dot), crinkle balls.
- **Capture personality:** The stretch, the yawn, the murder eyes, the loaf position. Cat owners love the real moments.

## Shot List — Pet Session
1. Classic portrait — sitting, looking at camera, ears up
2. Close-up face — eyes sharp, personality showing
3. Action — running, jumping, playing
4. With owner — in their arms, walking together, nose kiss
5. Detail shots — paws, nose, collar/tag, fur texture
6. Candid — sniffing, exploring, being themselves
7. The goofy shot — tongue out, mid-shake, zoomies. These are the ones that sell.

## Technical Tips
- **Focus on the eyes.** If the eyes aren't sharp, the photo doesn't work. Animal eye AF is a game-changer (Sony, Canon, Nikon all have it now).
- **Fast shutter speed:** 1/500 minimum for sitting pets, 1/1000+ for action.
- **Aperture:** f/2.8-4 for single animals (shallow DOF, subject separation). f/5.6-8 for multiple animals or animal + human.
- **Background:** Simple, uncluttered. Green grass, fall leaves, studio backdrop. The animal should pop.
- **Treats in your pocket, not your hand.** You need both hands for the camera.

## Safety
- Never let a dog off-leash in an unfenced area unless the owner confirms perfect recall.
- Watch for signs of stress: panting, whale eye, tucked tail, lip licking. Take a break.
- Carry water and a portable bowl.
- Ask about other animal interactions before bringing multiple pets together.
- Have treats but ask about allergies/dietary restrictions first.
