# Study Skills and Learning Strategies

## How to Study (Not Just "Read It Again")

### Active Recall (Best Method)
Don't just re-read notes. CLOSE the book and try to remember.
- Cover the answers and quiz yourself
- Write what you remember on a blank page
- Explain the concept out loud (to a pet, a stuffed animal, or the wall)
- If you can explain it, you know it. If you can't, that's what to study.

### Spaced Repetition
Don't cram everything the night before. Review a little bit each day:
- Day 1: Learn it
- Day 2: Quick review (5 min)
- Day 4: Review again
- Day 7: Review again
- Test day: You already know it!

**Explain it as:** "Your brain is like a path through grass. Walk it once, the grass bounces back. Walk it every day, and you get a clear trail."

### Flashcards
Great for math facts, vocabulary, science terms.
- One question per card
- Say the answer BEFORE flipping
- Sort into "know it" and "still learning" piles
- Focus time on the "still learning" pile
- Apps: Quizlet (free), or just index cards

### Practice Problems (Math)
Reading about math doesn't help. DOING math does.
- Work through problems without looking at the answer
- Check your work after each problem
- When you get one wrong, figure out WHERE you went wrong, not just what the right answer is
- Do the hardest problems, not just the easy ones

## Homework Strategies

### The Homework Routine
1. **Same time, same place** every day. Make it automatic.
2. **Remove distractions** — phone in another room, TV off.
3. **Start with the hardest subject** while your brain is fresh.
4. **Take breaks** — 25 minutes of work, 5 minutes of break (Pomodoro technique).
5. **Check your work** before calling it done.

### When You're Stuck
1. Re-read the directions (seriously — most "stuck" moments are from not reading carefully)
2. Look at a similar example in your textbook or notes
3. Try working backwards from the answer
4. Skip it and come back later — sometimes your brain solves it in the background
5. Ask for help (parent, friend, teacher, Sparky!)

**Don't:** Copy someone else's work. You won't learn it, and you'll be lost on the test.

## Test Preparation

### Before the Test
- Start studying 3-5 days ahead, not the night before
- Make a study guide: key concepts, formulas, vocabulary
- Do practice problems (especially for math and science)
- Get a good night's sleep (seriously — sleep helps memory)
- Eat breakfast

### During the Test
1. **Read ALL directions** first
2. **Skim the whole test** — see how many questions, budget your time
3. **Answer the easy ones first** — builds confidence, secures points
4. **Skip hard ones** — come back to them
5. **Show your work** (math) — you may get partial credit
6. **Eliminate wrong answers** (multiple choice) — narrow it down
7. **Check your work** if time allows
8. **Don't change answers** unless you're sure — first instinct is usually right

### Test Anxiety
It's normal to feel nervous. Here's what helps:
- **Breathe:** 4 counts in, hold 4, out 4. Repeat 3 times.
- **Positive self-talk:** "I studied. I know this. I'll do my best."
- **Remember:** One test doesn't define you. It's just one snapshot.
- **Prepare well** — confidence comes from knowing you're ready.

## Math-Specific Study Tips

### Times Tables
- Practice 5 minutes daily (not 30 minutes once a week)
- Use skip counting, songs, games
- Focus on the tricky ones: 6×7, 7×8, 8×6, 6×9, 7×9, 8×9
- Once you know the easy ones, ONLY drill the hard ones

### Word Problems
- Read the whole problem twice
- Draw a picture or diagram
- Use the CUBES method (Circle, Underline, Box, Evaluate, Solve)
- Check: does your answer make sense? (If 200 kids share 10 pizzas and you got 200 slices each, something's wrong)

### Showing Work
Not optional. Even if you can do it in your head:
- Write down each step
- Teachers can see where you went wrong (and give partial credit)
- YOU can see where you went wrong when checking
- It's proof that you actually did it

## Building Confidence

### Growth Mindset
- "I can't do this" → "I can't do this YET"
- Mistakes aren't failures — they're how you learn
- Being stuck means you're learning something new
- Smart isn't something you ARE — it's something you BECOME through practice

### Celebrate Progress
- Compare yourself to YOUR past self, not other students
- "Last week I got 3 right. This week I got 6 right. That's DOUBLE."
- Small wins add up to big improvements

### Mistakes Are Data
When you get something wrong:
1. Don't erase it — understand it
2. Where exactly did you go wrong?
3. What would you do differently?
4. Try a similar problem to make sure you've got it now

**Explain it as:** "Scientists do hundreds of experiments that 'fail' before they find what works. Each failure is just one more way they know doesn't work. That's not failure — that's progress."
