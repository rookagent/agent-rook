# Schedule C — Profit or Loss from Business (IRS Form 1040)

## Who Files Schedule C
- Sole proprietors (including single-member LLCs that haven't elected S-Corp treatment)
- Independent contractors who receive 1099-NEC
- Freelancers, gig workers, side hustlers
- Anyone with self-employment income not reported on W-2

You file Schedule C as part of your personal 1040. The net profit (or loss) flows to your 1040 Line 8 and is also subject to self-employment tax on Schedule SE.

## Part I — Income (Lines 1-7)

### Line 1: Gross Receipts
Total revenue from your business before any deductions. This includes:
- All 1099-NEC payments received
- Cash payments (even if no 1099 was issued)
- Credit card payments
- Bartered goods/services at fair market value
- **You must report ALL income**, not just what's on 1099s. The IRS matches 1099s to your return — missing income is the #1 audit trigger.

### Line 2: Returns and Allowances
Refunds, credits, or discounts you gave customers. Subtracted from gross receipts.

### Line 4: Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
Only for businesses that sell physical products. Calculated on the back of Schedule C (Part III).
- Materials, inventory purchases, shipping to you, direct labor for production
- NOT: office supplies, marketing, or services

### Line 6: Other Income
- Scrap/waste sales, recovered bad debts, interest on business accounts
- State/local tax refunds if deducted in prior year

### Line 7: Gross Income
Line 1 - Line 2 - Line 4 + Line 6 = Your gross income

## Part II — Expenses (Lines 8-27)

This is where deductions happen. Every dollar here reduces your taxable income.

### Line 8: Advertising
Marketing, website hosting, social media ads, business cards, promotional materials, SEO services. Fully deductible.

### Line 9: Car and Truck Expenses
Two methods — choose one and stick with it for the life of the vehicle:
1. **Standard Mileage Rate:** 67 cents/mile (2024). Track every business mile. Keep a mileage log.
2. **Actual Expenses:** Gas, insurance, repairs, depreciation, registration — prorated by business use percentage.

Commuting (home to regular office) is NEVER deductible. Home to client site IS deductible if your home is your principal place of business.

### Line 10: Commissions and Fees
Payments to subcontractors, referral fees, sales commissions. Issue 1099-NEC to anyone you pay $600+ in a year.

### Line 11: Contract Labor
Payments to independent contractors for services. Different from Line 26 (wages to employees).

### Line 12: Depletion
Rare — mainly for mining, oil, gas, or timber businesses.

### Line 13: Depreciation and Section 179
See the Depreciation knowledge module for deep detail. This covers:
- Section 179 immediate expensing (up to $1,220,000 in 2024)
- Bonus depreciation (60% in 2024, phasing down)
- MACRS standard depreciation schedules
- Computers, equipment, vehicles, furniture

### Line 14: Employee Benefit Programs
Health insurance for employees (not yourself — that goes on 1040 Line 17), retirement contributions for employees, group life insurance.

### Line 15: Insurance (Business)
General liability, professional liability (E&O), workers' comp, business property insurance, cyber liability. NOT health insurance for yourself.

### Line 16a: Mortgage Interest (Business Property)
Only if you own business property (not home office — that's separate).

### Line 16b: Other Interest
Business credit card interest, business loan interest, line of credit interest.

### Line 17: Legal and Professional Services
Attorney fees, CPA/accountant fees, bookkeeper fees, tax preparation, consulting. Must be business-related.

### Line 18: Office Expense
Office supplies, postage, printing, small equipment under $2,500 (de minimis safe harbor). Larger items go to Line 13 (depreciation).

### Line 19: Pension and Profit-Sharing Plans
Your own SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, or Solo 401(k) contributions. This is a powerful deduction — see Retirement module.

### Line 20a-b: Rent
- 20a: Vehicle/machinery/equipment rental
- 20b: Office space, coworking space, studio rental

### Line 21: Repairs and Maintenance
Keeping existing assets in working condition. Paint, cleaning, routine maintenance, minor repairs. NOT improvements (those get depreciated).

### Line 22: Supplies
Items consumed in the operation of your business. Not inventory (that's COGS). Think: cleaning supplies, tools, raw materials for services.

### Line 23: Taxes and Licenses
Business licenses, permits, state/local business taxes, employer's share of payroll taxes, personal property tax on business assets. NOT federal income tax or self-employment tax.

### Line 24a-b: Travel
Overnight business travel: airfare, hotel, taxi/rideshare, tips, baggage, laundry while traveling. NOT commuting. Meals while traveling are 50% deductible (separate from Line 24b).

### Line 25: Utilities
Electricity, gas, water, phone, internet — prorated by business use if shared with personal. If you have a dedicated business phone line, 100% deductible.

### Line 26: Wages
W-2 employee wages. If you're a sole proprietor, you do NOT pay yourself wages — your draw comes from net profit.

### Line 27a: Other Expenses
Anything legitimate that doesn't fit Lines 8-26. Common examples:
- Software subscriptions (QuickBooks, Adobe, Canva)
- Professional development, courses, conferences
- Bank fees, merchant processing fees (Stripe, Square, PayPal)
- Membership dues (professional associations, not clubs)
- Trade publications
- Bad debts (using accrual method only)

## Part III — Cost of Goods Sold (Lines 33-42)
Only for product sellers. Calculate: Beginning inventory + Purchases + Labor + Materials - Ending inventory = COGS.

## Part IV — Vehicle Information (Lines 43-47)
Required if you deducted vehicle expenses. Date placed in service, total miles, business miles, commuting miles, personal miles.

## Part V — Other Expenses (Line 48)
Itemize everything from Line 27a here.

## The Bottom Line — Net Profit or Loss (Line 31)
Gross Income (Line 7) - Total Expenses (Lines 28) - Business Use of Home (Line 30) = Net Profit.

This number goes to:
- **1040 Line 8** — taxed as ordinary income
- **Schedule SE** — subject to 15.3% self-employment tax (Social Security 12.4% + Medicare 2.9%)
- **Schedule 1 Line 15** — deductible portion of SE tax (50% of SE tax)

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not reporting all income** — IRS matches 1099s. If you got paid in cash, it's still income.
2. **Mixing personal and business expenses** — use a separate bank account and credit card.
3. **No documentation** — receipts, mileage logs, and records are required for every deduction.
4. **Deducting personal expenses as business** — the IRS knows what a Netflix subscription is.
5. **Filing Schedule C for a hobby** — if you don't have a profit motive, it's a hobby (Schedule 1, no deductions).

## Audit Risk Factors
- Net losses for 3+ of 5 consecutive years (hobby loss rule)
- Cash-intensive businesses with no paper trail
- Unusually high deductions relative to income
- Vehicle deduction claiming 100% business use
- Home office deduction on a $0-profit return
- Round numbers on every line (looks estimated, not tracked)
