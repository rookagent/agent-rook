# Business Entity Types & Tax Implications

## Sole Proprietorship (Default)

### What It Is
No formal registration required (beyond a business license in most states). You ARE the business. If you're freelancing, contracting, or side hustling without forming an entity, you're a sole proprietor.

### How It's Taxed
- File Schedule C with your personal 1040
- Net profit is subject to income tax AND self-employment tax (15.3%)
- No separation between personal and business assets for liability purposes
- No payroll — owner's draw, not salary

### Pros
- Simplest to set up and maintain (no state filings, no annual reports)
- Lowest administrative cost
- Full control

### Cons
- Unlimited personal liability (your personal assets are exposed)
- All net profit subject to SE tax
- Harder to raise investment capital
- Looks less professional to some clients

### Best For
Side hustlers, new freelancers, very small businesses. If you earn under $40,000/year in net profit and have low liability risk, this is fine.

## Single-Member LLC

### What It Is
A formal business entity registered with your state. Provides limited liability — your personal assets (house, savings, car) are protected from business debts and lawsuits (with exceptions for fraud, personal guarantees, and commingling funds).

### How It's Taxed (Default)
- **Disregarded entity** — the IRS ignores the LLC for tax purposes
- You still file Schedule C, same as a sole proprietor
- Same SE tax treatment
- The LLC provides LIABILITY protection, not TAX benefits

### How to Form
1. Choose a name (check availability with your state)
2. File Articles of Organization with your state (fee: $50-500 depending on state)
3. Get an EIN from the IRS (free, irs.gov, takes 5 minutes)
4. Open a business bank account (CRITICAL — do not commingle)
5. Check local business license requirements

### Annual Requirements
- Annual report or statement of information (most states, $0-800/year)
- California: $800 franchise tax minimum, regardless of income (ouch)
- Maintain separate finances — commingling pierces the liability veil

### Best For
Any business with liability risk, client-facing work, or net profit over $40,000. Low cost for significant protection.

## LLC Taxed as S-Corporation (Form 2553 Election)

### What It Is
Your LLC stays an LLC legally, but you ELECT to be taxed as an S-Corp by filing Form 2553 with the IRS. This changes how your income is taxed — not your legal structure.

### How It's Taxed
- You pay yourself a **reasonable salary** via W-2 payroll
- Remaining profit passes through as a **distribution** (not subject to SE tax)
- File Form 1120-S (S-Corp return) + Schedule K-1 → flows to your personal 1040

### The Tax Savings
Self-employment tax is 15.3% on ALL net profit as a sole proprietor. With S-Corp election, you only pay payroll taxes (equivalent to SE tax) on your SALARY, not on distributions.

**Example:**
- Net profit: $120,000
- Sole proprietor SE tax: $120,000 × 15.3% = $18,360
- S-Corp: Pay yourself $70,000 salary → payroll tax: $70,000 × 15.3% = $10,710
- Distribution: $50,000 → no SE tax
- **Savings: $7,650/year**

### "Reasonable Salary" Rules
The IRS requires your salary to be "reasonable" — comparable to what you'd pay someone else to do your job. Paying yourself $20,000 as a full-time software developer earning $150,000 will get flagged.

Factors:
- Industry norms for your role
- Your experience and qualifications
- Hours worked
- Revenue and profitability
- Comparable salaries on Glassdoor/Payscale

**Rule of thumb:** Salary should be 40-60% of net profit, depending on industry.

### When S-Corp Makes Sense
- Net profit consistently above $50,000-60,000/year
- Tax savings exceed the additional costs (payroll service, extra tax return)
- You can pay yourself a reasonable salary

### Additional Costs
- Payroll service: $40-100/month (Gusto, ADP, or your accountant)
- S-Corp tax return (Form 1120-S): $500-1,500/year to prepare
- Quarterly payroll tax deposits
- State requirements vary

### When S-Corp Does NOT Make Sense
- Net profit under $50,000 (savings don't justify costs)
- Highly variable income (hard to set consistent salary)
- You want simplicity over savings
- Single-year project (setup costs outweigh one year of savings)

### How to Elect
- File Form 2553 within 75 days of the start of the tax year you want it to take effect
- Can also elect with a late filing (within 3 years, 75 days) under Rev. Proc. 2013-30
- Election is with the IRS — your state may or may not recognize it

## C-Corporation

### What It Is
A separate legal entity that files its own tax return (Form 1120). The corporation pays corporate tax on its profits.

### How It's Taxed
- Corporate tax rate: flat 21% (TCJA)
- Dividends to shareholders are taxed again at shareholder's personal rate (15-20%)
- This is "double taxation"
- Salaries to owner-employees are deductible to the corporation

### When It Makes Sense
- You want to raise venture capital (investors prefer C-Corps)
- You need stock options for employees
- You plan to retain significant earnings in the business (21% corporate rate < personal rates)
- You want to go public eventually

### When It Doesn't
- Small service businesses (double taxation hurts)
- Solo operators (S-Corp or LLC is almost always better)
- Businesses that distribute most profits to owners

## Partnership / Multi-Member LLC

### How It's Taxed
- File Form 1065 (partnership return) — informational only, partnership doesn't pay tax
- Each partner gets Schedule K-1 showing their share of income/loss
- Partners pay income tax + SE tax on their share
- Operating agreement determines profit/loss split

### Key Issues
- Each partner's SE tax is based on their distributive share, not distributions
- Partners cannot be W-2 employees of the partnership
- Must have a written operating agreement (determines profit splits, management, exit terms)

## Comparison Table

| | Sole Prop | LLC | S-Corp (LLC) | C-Corp |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Liability protection | No | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| SE tax on all profit | Yes | Yes | No (salary only) | No |
| Separate tax return | No (Sched C) | No (Sched C) | Yes (1120-S) | Yes (1120) |
| Annual state fees | None | $0-800 | $0-800 | $0-800+ |
| Payroll required | No | No | Yes | Yes |
| Complexity | Very low | Low | Medium | High |
| Best for income | <$40K | $40-60K | >$60K | VC-backed |

## EIN (Employer Identification Number)

Every business except sole proprietors without employees CAN get an EIN. LLCs, S-Corps, and C-Corps MUST have one.

- Free from the IRS: irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/apply-for-an-employer-identification-number-ein-online
- Takes 5 minutes online
- Use it for bank accounts, tax filings, and 1099s instead of your SSN
- Even sole proprietors should get one to avoid sharing their SSN

## When to Change Entity Type
1. **Sole prop → LLC:** When you have clients, liability risk, or want professionalism. Do it early — it's cheap.
2. **LLC → S-Corp election:** When net profit exceeds $50-60K consistently and payroll costs are justified.
3. **S-Corp → C-Corp:** When raising VC money or planning IPO. Rare for small businesses.

Always consult a CPA before changing entity type. The tax implications depend on your specific situation, state, and timing.
