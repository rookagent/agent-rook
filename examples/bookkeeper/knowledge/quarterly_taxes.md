# Quarterly Estimated Taxes (Form 1040-ES)

## Who Must Pay Quarterly
You must make estimated tax payments if you expect to owe **$1,000 or more** in federal tax after subtracting withholding and credits. This applies to:
- Sole proprietors / freelancers
- Independent contractors
- Partners in a partnership
- S-Corp shareholders with non-wage income
- Anyone with significant investment income, rental income, or alimony received

If you have a W-2 job that withholds taxes AND self-employment income, you may be able to increase W-2 withholding to cover the SE tax instead of making quarterly payments. Use Form W-4 to adjust.

## Payment Deadlines (Calendar Year Filers)

| Quarter | Income Earned | Payment Due |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| Q1 | Jan 1 – Mar 31 | April 15 |
| Q2 | Apr 1 – May 31 | June 15 |
| Q3 | Jun 1 – Aug 31 | September 15 |
| Q4 | Sep 1 – Dec 31 | January 15 (following year) |

If the due date falls on a weekend or holiday, the deadline moves to the next business day.

**Important:** Q2 only covers 2 months of income but Q3 covers 3 months. This catches many people off guard — Q3 payments should be larger than Q2.

## How Much to Pay — Two Safe Harbor Methods

If you meet either safe harbor, you avoid the underpayment penalty even if you owe a balance at filing.

### Method 1: 100% of Prior Year Tax (110% if AGI > $150,000)
Pay at least 100% of your **prior year total tax liability** (line 24 on Form 1040), spread across 4 equal payments. If your AGI exceeded $150,000 ($75,000 MFS), the threshold is 110%.

**Best for:** Rapidly growing businesses. Even if you earn 3x more this year, you're safe if you paid last year's amount.

### Method 2: 90% of Current Year Tax
Pay at least 90% of your **current year expected tax liability**, spread across 4 payments.

**Best for:** Declining income years. If you earned less this year, you pay based on what you actually owe.

### Practical Approach
Most bookkeepers recommend the **prior year safe harbor** because it's a known number. Calculate it once in January, divide by 4, set calendar reminders.

## How to Calculate Your Estimated Tax

### Step 1: Estimate Net Self-Employment Income
Revenue - Business Expenses = Net Profit (Schedule C Line 31)

### Step 2: Calculate Self-Employment Tax
Net Profit × 92.35% × 15.3% = SE Tax
(The 92.35% accounts for the employer-equivalent portion that's deductible)

For 2024:
- Social Security: 12.4% on first $168,600 of combined wages + SE income
- Medicare: 2.9% on ALL SE income (no cap)
- Additional Medicare: 0.9% on SE income above $200,000 ($250,000 MFJ)

### Step 3: Calculate Income Tax
(Net Profit - 50% of SE Tax - Standard Deduction - Other Deductions) × Your Marginal Tax Rate

2024 Federal Tax Brackets (Single):
| Taxable Income | Rate |
|---------------|------|
| $0 – $11,600 | 10% |
| $11,601 – $47,150 | 12% |
| $47,151 – $100,525 | 22% |
| $100,526 – $191,950 | 24% |
| $191,951 – $243,725 | 32% |
| $243,726 – $609,350 | 35% |
| Over $609,350 | 37% |

### Step 4: Total Estimated Tax
Income Tax + SE Tax - Credits - W-2 Withholding = Annual Estimated Tax Due

Divide by 4 for quarterly payments.

### Quick Rule of Thumb
Set aside **25-30% of net profit** for taxes. This covers both income tax and self-employment tax for most small business owners in the 22-24% bracket.

## How to Pay

### IRS Direct Pay (Recommended)
- pay.irs.gov — free, instant, from your bank account
- Select "Estimated Tax" → "1040-ES" → tax year → quarter

### EFTPS (Electronic Federal Tax Payment System)
- eftps.gov — requires enrollment (takes 5-7 days)
- Preferred for businesses that make recurring payments
- Can schedule payments in advance

### IRS2Go App
Mobile payments via Direct Pay

### Mail a Check
Mail Form 1040-ES voucher with check to the IRS address for your state. Slowest method — allows for lost mail and processing delays.

### Credit/Debit Card
Third-party processors (pay1040.com, payusatax.com). Convenience fees apply (1.85-1.98% for credit, $2.50-2.69 flat for debit). Only use this if you need credit card rewards or float.

## State Estimated Taxes
Most states with income tax also require quarterly estimated payments. Deadlines usually match federal but some differ. Check your state's Department of Revenue.

States with NO income tax: AK, FL, NH (limited), NV, SD, TN (limited), TX, WA, WY.

## Underpayment Penalty (Form 2210)
If you don't meet either safe harbor AND owe $1,000+ at filing, you owe a penalty.

- Penalty rate = IRS interest rate (currently ~8% annually, compounded daily)
- Calculated per quarter, not annually — so missing Q1 costs more than missing Q4
- The penalty is NOT dischargeable in bankruptcy

### When the Penalty Is Waived
- Tax owed is less than $1,000 after withholding and credits
- You paid 100%/110% of prior year tax (safe harbor 1)
- You paid 90% of current year tax (safe harbor 2)
- You had no tax liability in the prior year (and were a US citizen/resident for all 12 months)
- Casualty, disaster, or other unusual circumstances
- You retired after age 62 or became disabled during the year

## Annualized Income Installment Method
If your income is uneven (seasonal business, large Q4 project), you can use Form 2210 Schedule AI to calculate required payments based on income actually earned in each period rather than flat 25% quarterly. This avoids penalties for uneven income.

## Year-End True-Up
After December 31, do a final calculation:
1. Add up actual net profit for the year
2. Calculate actual total tax liability
3. Subtract all quarterly payments made + any W-2 withholding
4. If you owe: file and pay by April 15 (no penalty if safe harbor met)
5. If overpaid: claim refund or apply to next year's Q1

## Common Mistakes
1. **Not paying quarterly at all** — "I'll just pay it all at tax time." The penalty for this is real money.
2. **Using current year method with volatile income** — use prior year safe harbor for predictability.
3. **Forgetting state payments** — federal AND state have separate estimated tax requirements.
4. **Not adjusting when income changes significantly** — if you get a big contract mid-year, adjust remaining payments up.
5. **Missing the January 15 Q4 deadline** — if you file and pay in full by January 31, the Q4 payment is waived (but tight window).
