# Year-End Tax Planning for Small Business

## The Big Idea
Tax planning isn't April 15 — it's October through December. By year-end, you know your approximate income, and you have a narrow window to take actions that reduce your tax bill legally.

## Strategy 1: Accelerate Expenses

### How It Works (Cash Basis)
Pay deductible expenses before December 31 to reduce this year's income:
- **Prepay January rent** in December
- **Buy equipment/supplies** you'll need in Q1
- **Stock up on inventory** (if you sell products)
- **Renew annual subscriptions** early (software, insurance, memberships)
- **Make retirement contributions** (SEP-IRA, Solo 401k)
- **Pay contractor invoices** before year-end instead of January

### When to Use This
When your income is HIGHER this year than you expect next year. Move expenses into the high-income year for maximum deduction value.

## Strategy 2: Defer Income

### How It Works (Cash Basis)
Delay receiving income until January:
- **Invoice in late December** knowing payment will arrive in January
- **Delay project completion** until early January (if feasible)
- **Hold depositing checks** until January 1 (risky — must be reasonable)

### When to Use This
When your income is higher this year and you expect a lower-income year ahead. Push income into the year where it'll be taxed at a lower rate.

### Caution
- Don't delay income artificially — the IRS constructive receipt doctrine means income you COULD have received counts as received
- If a client mails a check December 28 and it arrives December 30, it's 2024 income even if you don't deposit until January

## Strategy 3: Section 179 / Bonus Depreciation Timing

### Before December 31
If you're buying equipment, vehicles, or technology, place them in service before December 31 to claim:
- **Section 179** immediate deduction (up to $1,220,000)
- **Bonus depreciation** (60% in 2024)
- **De minimis safe harbor** for items under $2,500

### The December Trap
Buying a $50,000 vehicle on December 30 gets you the SAME Section 179 deduction as buying it on January 2 — but if you use the standard MACRS depreciation, the half-year convention gives you only half a year of depreciation for December placement. Bonus depreciation avoids this issue.

## Strategy 4: Maximize Retirement Contributions

### SEP-IRA
- Contribute up to 25% of net SE income (after SE tax deduction)
- **Deadline: Tax filing date** (April 15, or October 15 with extension)
- You can decide the exact amount AFTER year-end — maximum flexibility

### Solo 401(k)
- **Employee contribution ($23,000):** Must be made by December 31 (or you lose it)
- **Employer contribution (up to 25%):** Deadline is tax filing date
- Can do both Roth and traditional on the employee side

### SIMPLE IRA
- Employee contribution deadline: January 30 following the year (30 days after year-end)
- Employer match/contribution: Tax filing date

### Strategy
Max out employee contributions by December 31 (Solo 401k, SIMPLE). Decide employer contributions after you know your final numbers.

## Strategy 5: Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction — Section 199A

### The Deduction
Sole proprietors, S-Corp owners, and partners may deduct up to **20% of qualified business income** from their taxable income. This is huge — it effectively reduces your top tax rate on business income by 20%.

### Simple Calculation (Taxable Income Under $191,950 Single / $383,900 MFJ)
QBI Deduction = 20% × Qualified Business Income

### Phase-Out and Limitations (Income Above Thresholds)
Above the income threshold, limitations kick in:
1. **Specified Service Trades or Businesses (SSTB):** Health, law, accounting, consulting, financial services, athletics, performing arts — the deduction phases out entirely
2. **Non-SSTB businesses:** Deduction limited to the greater of:
   - 50% of W-2 wages paid by the business, OR
   - 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of the unadjusted basis of qualified property

### Planning Implications
- **Under the threshold?** You get the full 20% — no planning needed
- **Near the threshold?** Consider deferring income or accelerating deductions to stay below
- **SSTB over threshold?** Consider restructuring (splitting business activities)
- **Non-SSTB over threshold?** W-2 wages matter — for S-Corp owners, reasonable salary feeds the QBI calculation

## Strategy 6: Estimated Tax True-Up

### Before January 15
- Calculate your actual total tax liability for the year
- Determine if you've met safe harbor (100%/110% of prior year tax)
- If short, make a Q4 estimated payment by January 15
- Or file your return by January 31 and pay in full (waives Q4 payment)

### Overpayment
If you overpaid estimates, you can:
- Get a refund
- Apply the overpayment to next year's Q1 estimated tax

## Year-End Checklist

### October-November
- [ ] Review year-to-date P&L — where are you vs. last year?
- [ ] Estimate total annual income and tax liability
- [ ] Identify large purchases to make before Dec 31 (Section 179)
- [ ] Review contractor payments — anyone approaching $600? Collect W-9s
- [ ] Schedule year-end meeting with CPA/bookkeeper

### December
- [ ] Accelerate expenses if income is higher than expected
- [ ] Make Solo 401(k) employee contributions by Dec 31
- [ ] Buy needed equipment/supplies before Dec 31
- [ ] Send all outstanding invoices (or defer if deferring income)
- [ ] Prepay January expenses if beneficial
- [ ] Make Q4 estimated tax payment if needed
- [ ] Donate to charity if itemizing (cash by Dec 31, stock by Dec 31)
- [ ] Organize receipts and documentation for the year
- [ ] Reconcile all accounts through December 31

### January
- [ ] File and pay Q4 estimated tax by January 15 (or file full return by Jan 31)
- [ ] Issue all 1099-NECs by January 31
- [ ] Issue W-2s by January 31 (if you have employees)
- [ ] File Form 941 (Q4 payroll) by January 31
- [ ] File Form 940 (FUTA) by January 31
- [ ] Make SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) employer contribution (by filing deadline)

## The 3-Year Tax Planning View

Don't plan for one year in isolation. Consider:
- Am I expecting a big income year next year? (Save deductions)
- Am I expecting a slow year? (Take deductions now)
- Am I nearing the QBI phase-out threshold? (Manage AGI)
- Should I convert to S-Corp next year? (Compare SE tax savings vs. costs)
- Is my entity type still optimal? (Review every 2-3 years)

The best tax planning is proactive and multi-year. The worst is reactive and April 14.
