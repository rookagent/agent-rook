# Depreciation, Section 179, and Bonus Depreciation

## The Concept
When you buy a business asset that lasts more than one year (equipment, vehicles, furniture, computers), you generally can't deduct the full cost in the year you buy it. Instead, you spread the deduction over the asset's "useful life" — this is depreciation.

BUT — Congress has created accelerated methods that let you deduct most or all of the cost immediately. These are the three key mechanisms:

## 1. De Minimis Safe Harbor (Expense Immediately)

### The Rule
Items costing **$2,500 or less per invoice** (or per item) can be expensed in full in the year purchased, rather than depreciated.

### Requirements
- Must have a written accounting policy in place at the start of the tax year
- Must apply consistently to all qualifying expenditures
- Elect on your tax return each year

### What Qualifies
- Laptops, monitors, keyboards, mice
- Phones, tablets
- Small tools, equipment
- Office furniture under the threshold
- Printers, scanners

### What Doesn't
- Items over $2,500 (goes to Section 179, bonus depreciation, or MACRS)
- Land (never depreciable)
- Inventory (goes to COGS)

### For Audited Financial Statements
If you have an AFS (audited financial statement), the threshold increases to $5,000 per item.

## 2. Section 179 Deduction (Form 4562, Part I)

### The Rule
Deduct the FULL cost of qualifying business property in the year it's placed in service, instead of depreciating over years.

### 2024 Limits
- **Maximum deduction:** $1,220,000
- **Phase-out begins:** When total qualifying property placed in service exceeds $3,050,000
- **Dollar-for-dollar reduction** above the phase-out threshold
- Limits adjust annually for inflation

### What Qualifies
- Tangible personal property (equipment, machinery, tools)
- Office furniture and fixtures
- Computers, software (off-the-shelf)
- Vehicles (with special limits — see below)
- Certain qualified real property improvements:
  - Roofs, HVAC, fire protection, alarm/security systems
  - Interior improvements to nonresidential buildings

### What Does NOT Qualify
- Buildings and structural components (unless qualified improvement)
- Land
- Property used outside the US
- Property acquired from related parties
- Property used 50% or less for business
- Air conditioning or heating units (if structural)

### Key Limitation
**Cannot create or increase a net loss.** Your Section 179 deduction is limited to your taxable business income. Unused amounts carry forward to future years.

Example: Net profit before Section 179 = $30,000. You buy $50,000 in equipment. You can deduct $30,000 under Section 179 this year and carry forward $20,000 to next year.

### Vehicle Limits (Section 179 + Bonus)
Passenger vehicles have special annual caps:
- **Year 1:** $20,400 (with bonus) or $12,400 (without bonus) — 2024
- **Year 2:** $19,800
- **Year 3:** $11,900
- **Year 4+:** $7,160

**SUVs over 6,000 lbs GVWR:** Section 179 limit of $28,900 (2024). Not subject to annual passenger vehicle caps — but subject to the overall Section 179 limit. This is the "SUV loophole."

**Vehicles over 6,000 lbs GVWR that are NOT SUVs** (pickup trucks with 6-foot bed, cargo vans, box trucks): No special limitation — full Section 179 up to the $1,220,000 limit.

## 3. Bonus Depreciation (Form 4562, Part II)

### The Rule
Deduct a percentage of the cost of qualifying NEW and USED business property in the first year.

### Phase-Down Schedule
| Year Property Placed in Service | Bonus % |
|------|------|
| 2022 | 100% |
| 2023 | 80% |
| 2024 | 60% |
| 2025 | 40% |
| 2026 | 20% |
| 2027+ | 0% |

### Key Differences from Section 179
- **CAN create a net loss** (Section 179 cannot)
- Applies to NEW and USED property (since TCJA 2017)
- No annual dollar limit (Section 179 caps at $1,220,000)
- No income limitation
- Applies automatically unless you elect out

### Electing Out
You can elect out of bonus depreciation on a class-by-class basis. You might do this if:
- You want to spread the deduction over multiple years (income smoothing)
- You're in a low tax bracket this year and expect higher income later
- You want to preserve the deduction for a year with higher tax liability

## 4. MACRS — Regular Depreciation

If you don't use Section 179 or bonus depreciation (or for the portion not covered), you depreciate assets over their IRS-defined recovery period using MACRS (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System).

### Recovery Periods (Common Assets)

| Asset Type | Recovery Period | MACRS Method |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Computers, peripherals | 5 years | 200% DB |
| Office furniture | 7 years | 200% DB |
| Vehicles (cars, trucks) | 5 years | 200% DB |
| Office equipment | 5 years | 200% DB |
| Appliances | 5 years | 200% DB |
| Residential rental property | 27.5 years | Straight-line |
| Nonresidential real property | 39 years | Straight-line |
| Qualified improvement property | 15 years | Straight-line |
| Land improvements (fence, parking) | 15 years | 150% DB |

### Convention
- **Half-year convention** (default): Treat all property as placed in service at the midpoint of the year. First year you get half a year of depreciation.
- **Mid-quarter convention:** If more than 40% of your depreciable property is placed in service in Q4, you must use this convention (less favorable).

### 200% Declining Balance vs. Straight-Line
- 200% DB is accelerated — more deduction in early years, less later
- Switches to straight-line when it becomes more advantageous
- IRS depreciation tables in Publication 946 provide the exact percentages per year

## Strategy: Choosing the Right Method

### Priority Order (Usually)
1. **De minimis safe harbor** — items under $2,500, expense immediately
2. **Section 179** — items over $2,500, deduct in full (up to income limit)
3. **Bonus depreciation** — if Section 179 is exhausted or you want to create a loss
4. **MACRS** — everything else, or if you intentionally want to spread the deduction

### Planning Considerations
- **High income year?** Maximize Section 179 and bonus depreciation now
- **Expecting higher income next year?** Consider electing out of bonus, use MACRS to save deductions for later
- **Near the $168,600 Social Security wage base?** Taking depreciation reduces net SE income, potentially reducing Social Security benefits (usually worth it for tax savings, but know the tradeoff)

## Listed Property Rules

"Listed property" is property that can easily be used for personal purposes:
- Vehicles
- Computers (if not used exclusively at a regular business establishment)
- Cell phones (removed from listed property in 2010)

### The 50% Test
Listed property must be used MORE than 50% for business to qualify for Section 179 or bonus depreciation. If business use drops to 50% or below in any year, you must:
1. Switch to straight-line depreciation going forward
2. Recapture any excess depreciation claimed in prior years (report as income)

### Documentation
Keep a log of business vs. personal use for all listed property. For vehicles, this means a mileage log.

## Depreciation Recapture (When You Sell)

When you sell a depreciated asset for more than its book value (cost minus accumulated depreciation), the gain up to the amount of depreciation taken is taxed as ordinary income (not capital gains). This is Section 1245 recapture.

Example:
- Bought equipment for $10,000
- Depreciated $8,000 over 3 years (book value: $2,000)
- Sell for $6,000
- Gain: $4,000 ($6,000 - $2,000)
- ALL $4,000 is ordinary income (recapture of depreciation)

For real property (Section 1250), recapture is taxed at a maximum rate of 25%.

## Form 4562 — Depreciation and Amortization
- Part I: Section 179 election
- Part II: Bonus depreciation
- Part III: MACRS depreciation
- Part IV: Summary
- Part V: Listed property
- Part VI: Amortization (intangible assets like patents, trademarks — spread over 15 years)

File Form 4562 with your Schedule C if you're claiming any depreciation in the first year an asset is placed in service, or if you're claiming Section 179.
