# Sales Tax for Small Business

## The Basics
Sales tax is a **state and local tax** collected by businesses on taxable sales and remitted to the government. There is no federal sales tax.

**You are a collection agent, not the taxpayer.** You collect sales tax from customers and pass it to the state. Failing to collect when required means YOU owe the tax out of pocket.

## Who Must Collect Sales Tax

### Physical Nexus (Traditional)
You have nexus (tax obligation) in a state if you have a physical presence there:
- Office, warehouse, or retail location
- Employees or sales representatives
- Inventory stored (including Amazon FBA warehouses)
- Attending trade shows (some states, temporary nexus)

### Economic Nexus (Post-Wayfair, 2018)
After South Dakota v. Wayfair (2018), states can require remote sellers to collect sales tax based on economic activity alone:
- **Common threshold:** $100,000 in sales OR 200 transactions in the state per calendar year
- Each state sets its own threshold
- Applies to online sellers, SaaS companies, remote service providers

### When You Start Collecting
Once you exceed a state's nexus threshold, you must:
1. Register for a sales tax permit in that state
2. Begin collecting sales tax on taxable sales
3. File returns and remit collected tax on the required schedule

## What's Taxable (Varies by State!)

### Generally Taxable
- Physical products (retail goods, inventory)
- Some digital products (software, ebooks, digital music — varies by state)
- Prepared food (restaurants, catered meals)
- Lodging/accommodations

### Generally Exempt
- Groceries (in most states — some tax them)
- Prescription drugs
- Medical devices
- Resale purchases (buyer provides resale certificate)
- Sales to nonprofits (with exemption certificate)

### Services (The Messy Part)
**Most states do NOT tax services** — but it varies wildly:
- **Hawaii and New Mexico:** Tax almost all services
- **Texas, Ohio:** Tax some services (data processing, repair, pest control)
- **Most states:** Don't tax professional services (consulting, accounting, legal, design, photography)

**SaaS (Software as a Service):** Increasingly taxed. Currently taxable in ~20 states.

**Always check your specific state's rules** for your specific type of service or product.

## Sales Tax Rates

Sales tax is a combination of:
- **State rate** (0% to 7.25%)
- **County rate** (0% to ~3%)
- **City rate** (0% to ~5%)
- **Special district rate** (transit, stadium, etc.)

Total combined rates can exceed 10% in some jurisdictions.

**States with no sales tax:** Alaska (but localities can tax), Delaware, Montana, New Hampshire, Oregon.

### Origin vs. Destination Sourcing
- **Origin-based** (minority of states): Charge the rate where YOUR business is located
- **Destination-based** (majority of states): Charge the rate where the BUYER is located

Most states use destination-based sourcing, which means you need to calculate the correct rate for each customer's address.

## How to Collect

### Registration
Register for a sales tax permit with each state where you have nexus. Usually free, done online through the state's Department of Revenue website. **Never collect sales tax without a valid permit.**

### Calculation
- Determine if the sale is taxable in the buyer's state
- Look up the combined rate for the buyer's address
- Add sales tax to the invoice/receipt as a separate line item

### Tools
- **TaxJar** ($19-99/month) — AutoFile calculates, collects, and files in all states
- **Avalara** (custom pricing) — Enterprise-grade, integrates with major platforms
- **Manual lookup** — state websites have rate lookup tools (tedious for multi-state)

## How to Remit (File and Pay)

### Filing Frequency (Assigned by the State)
- **Monthly:** High-volume sellers (>$300/month in tax)
- **Quarterly:** Medium-volume ($100-300/month)
- **Annually:** Low-volume (under $100/month)
- **Semi-annually:** Some states offer this

Your filing frequency is typically assigned when you register, based on expected sales volume. It can change as your volume changes.

### Filing Returns
Each state has its own return form and portal. The return shows:
- Gross sales
- Exempt sales (with reason codes)
- Taxable sales
- Tax collected
- Tax due (should match tax collected if you're collecting correctly)

### Vendor Discount
Some states give you a small percentage of collected tax as compensation for your collection costs (usually 1-3% of tax due, capped).

## Resale Certificates

If a customer is buying your product to resell, they can provide a **resale certificate** (sometimes called exemption certificate) and avoid paying sales tax. You keep the certificate on file.

**Your responsibility:** Verify the certificate is valid, complete, and from a registered business. Keep it for 4+ years. If the state audits you and the certificate is missing or invalid, YOU owe the tax.

## Marketplace Facilitator Laws

If you sell through a marketplace (Amazon, Etsy, Shopify POS, eBay), the **marketplace is responsible** for collecting and remitting sales tax in most states. You do NOT collect additional sales tax on marketplace sales.

**Direct sales** (your own website, in-person, invoices) are still your responsibility.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not registering when you have nexus** — collecting without a permit is illegal in most states
2. **Not collecting when required** — you owe the tax out of pocket if you should have collected but didn't
3. **Charging the wrong rate** — use a lookup tool, not memory
4. **Filing late** — penalties and interest add up fast
5. **Ignoring economic nexus** — "I'm small" doesn't exempt you if you exceed the threshold
6. **Not keeping exemption certificates** — if audited, no certificate = you owe the tax
7. **Forgetting local taxes** — state tax is just the base; county and city taxes add on top
