# 1099 Rules — Contractor Payments and Reporting

## The Basic Rule
If you pay an **individual or non-corporate entity** $600 or more during the calendar year for **services performed in the course of your trade or business**, you must report it on Form 1099-NEC.

## Which Form?

### 1099-NEC (Nonemployee Compensation)
Used for: payments to independent contractors, freelancers, subcontractors for services.
- Web developer you hired: 1099-NEC
- Photographer's second shooter: 1099-NEC
- Bookkeeper you contracted: 1099-NEC
- Attorney fees: 1099-NEC

### 1099-MISC (Miscellaneous)
Used for: rent, prizes, royalties, other income payments, crop insurance proceeds, fishing boat proceeds, medical payments.
- Rent you pay for office space to an individual landlord: 1099-MISC Box 1
- Royalties: 1099-MISC Box 2
- NOT used for contractor payments (that's 1099-NEC now)

### 1099-K (Payment Card and Third Party Network)
Issued BY payment processors (PayPal, Venmo, Stripe, Square) when they process payments for you. You don't file this — they do. Threshold: $600 (lowered from $20,000/200 transactions).

## When You DON'T Need to File a 1099

- Payments to **corporations** (C-Corps and S-Corps) — except for attorney fees and medical/health payments
- Payments under **$600** in the calendar year
- Payments for **products/merchandise** (not services)
- Payments made via **credit card or payment processor** (reported on 1099-K instead)
- Payments to **employees** (reported on W-2)
- **Personal payments** (not related to your trade or business)
- Rent paid to a **real estate agent** or **property management company** (they're typically corporations)

### The Corporation Exception
You DO issue 1099s to corporations for:
- Attorney/legal fees (any amount over $600)
- Medical/health care payments
- Fish purchases for cash
- Substitute dividend/tax-exempt interest payments

## W-9 — Collect BEFORE You Pay

### What It Is
Form W-9 (Request for Taxpayer Identification Number) collects the contractor's:
- Legal name
- Business name (if different)
- Federal tax classification (individual, LLC, corporation, etc.)
- Taxpayer ID (SSN or EIN)
- Address

### When to Collect
**Before you make the first payment.** Make it part of your onboarding process. If they won't provide a W-9, you must withhold 24% of their payment as backup withholding (deposit with IRS).

### Storage
Keep W-9s for 4 years after the last return they relate to. Digital copies are fine.

## Filing Deadlines

| Form | Recipient Copy Due | IRS Filing Due (Paper) | IRS Filing Due (E-file) |
|------|-------------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| 1099-NEC | January 31 | January 31 | January 31 |
| 1099-MISC | January 31 | February 28 | March 31 |

**No extension available for 1099-NEC.** The January 31 deadline is firm.

## How to File

### Paper Filing (25 or fewer forms)
1. Get official IRS forms (Order from irs.gov or buy from office supply store — printed forms MUST be scannable, don't print from PDF)
2. Fill out Copy A (for IRS), Copy B (for recipient), Copy C (for your records)
3. Mail Copy A + Form 1096 (summary transmittal) to the IRS
4. Mail or deliver Copy B to the recipient

### E-filing (Required for 10+ forms starting 2024)
- IRS IRIS (Information Returns Intake System) — free, at irs.gov
- Third-party services: Tax1099.com, Track1099.com, eFile4Biz.com
- Most accounting software (QuickBooks, FreshBooks) can generate and e-file

### State Filing
Many states require 1099 filing too. Some participate in the Combined Federal/State Filing Program (IRS forwards to the state). Check your state requirements.

## Penalties for Not Filing

| Violation | Penalty Per Form |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Filed within 30 days late | $60 |
| Filed after 30 days but by August 1 | $130 |
| Filed after August 1 or not at all | $330 |
| Intentional disregard | $660 (no maximum) |

Maximum penalties apply per year based on business size. Small businesses (gross receipts ≤ $5 million): $220,500 max per year for the $60 penalty tier.

## Contractor vs. Employee — The Critical Distinction

Misclassifying an employee as a contractor is one of the IRS's top enforcement priorities. If they reclassify your contractors as employees, you owe:
- Back payroll taxes (employer's share of FICA: 7.65%)
- Penalties for failure to withhold
- Interest
- Potential state penalties (unemployment insurance, workers' comp)

### IRS Three-Factor Test

**1. Behavioral Control — Does the business control what the worker does and how?**
- **Employee indicators:** You set hours, provide training, dictate methods, require reports
- **Contractor indicators:** They choose when/where/how to work, use their own methods, provide their own training

**2. Financial Control — Does the business control the economic aspects?**
- **Employee indicators:** You provide tools/equipment, reimburse expenses, set pay rate, can hire/fire
- **Contractor indicators:** They have unreimbursed expenses, invest in their own tools, can profit or lose, have multiple clients

**3. Relationship Type — How do the parties perceive the relationship?**
- **Employee indicators:** Written contract says "employee," benefits provided, relationship is permanent, work is integral to business
- **Contractor indicators:** Written contract says "contractor," no benefits, project-based, specialized skill that's not your core business

### Bright Lines
No single factor is decisive. But these strongly indicate employee status:
- You set their schedule
- You provide all equipment
- They work exclusively for you
- The relationship is indefinite
- You train them in your methods

### Safe Harbor (Section 530)
If you have a "reasonable basis" for treating workers as contractors (prior IRS audit that accepted the classification, industry practice, court case, or IRS ruling), you may qualify for relief from reclassification penalties. But this doesn't protect you going forward.

## Best Practices

1. **Collect W-9 before first payment** — no exceptions
2. **Track all contractor payments throughout the year** — don't scramble in January
3. **Use accounting software** that flags when a contractor crosses $600
4. **File on time** — January 31 for 1099-NEC, no extensions
5. **Keep records** for 4 years
6. **Use written contracts** with every contractor specifying scope, deliverables, payment terms, and independent contractor status
7. **Don't treat contractors like employees** — no set hours, no required office presence, no company email
