# Bookkeeping Fundamentals

## Cash Basis vs. Accrual Basis

### Cash Basis (Most Common for Small Business)
- **Income recorded when received** (check deposited, payment hits bank)
- **Expenses recorded when paid** (bill paid, credit card charged)
- Simple, intuitive, matches your bank statement
- Required if gross receipts under $29 million (most small businesses)
- IRS default for sole proprietors

### Accrual Basis
- **Income recorded when earned** (invoice sent, even if unpaid)
- **Expenses recorded when incurred** (bill received, even if unpaid)
- More accurate picture of profitability
- Required for businesses with inventory over $29M or C-corporations
- Creates accounts receivable (money owed TO you) and accounts payable (money YOU owe)

### Which to Choose
Cash basis for most sole proprietors and freelancers. It's simpler, and you only pay tax on money you've actually received. Switch to accrual when your business has significant receivables/payables or you want a more accurate P&L.

**You cannot switch methods without IRS approval (Form 3115).**

## Chart of Accounts

Your chart of accounts is the list of all categories where money flows. Think of it as your filing system for transactions.

### Standard Categories

**Assets (what you own):**
- Checking Account
- Savings Account
- Accounts Receivable (accrual only)
- Equipment
- Vehicles
- Inventory

**Liabilities (what you owe):**
- Credit Card Balance
- Accounts Payable (accrual only)
- Business Loan
- Sales Tax Payable

**Equity (your stake):**
- Owner's Equity / Capital
- Owner's Draw (money you take out)
- Retained Earnings

**Income:**
- Service Revenue
- Product Sales
- Other Income (interest, refunds)

**Expenses (match Schedule C lines):**
- Advertising & Marketing
- Car & Truck Expenses
- Contract Labor
- Insurance
- Legal & Professional Fees
- Office Expenses & Supplies
- Rent
- Software & Subscriptions
- Travel
- Meals (50% deductible)
- Utilities
- Bank Fees & Merchant Fees
- Education & Training
- Equipment (under $2,500)

### Tips
- Don't over-complicate it. 15-25 expense categories is plenty for most small businesses.
- Match categories to Schedule C lines — makes tax prep trivial.
- Be consistent. If you categorize Zoom as "Software & Subscriptions" in January, do it that way in December too.

## Bank Reconciliation

Reconciliation means matching your books to your bank statement. Do this MONTHLY.

### Process
1. Pull your bank statement for the month (or connect your bank to your accounting software)
2. Match every transaction in your books to a transaction on the statement
3. Identify discrepancies:
   - Transactions in your books but not on the statement (outstanding checks, pending deposits)
   - Transactions on the statement but not in your books (bank fees, auto-payments you forgot)
4. Record any missing transactions
5. Your book balance should match your statement ending balance (adjusted for outstanding items)

### Why It Matters
- Catches errors (double entries, wrong amounts, missed transactions)
- Catches fraud (unauthorized charges)
- Ensures your financial statements are accurate
- Required for any audit or bank loan application

### How Often
- **Monthly:** Non-negotiable. Set a recurring calendar event.
- **Weekly:** Even better if you have high transaction volume.
- **Annually:** You're going to hate yourself at tax time. Don't do this.

## Financial Statements

### Income Statement (Profit & Loss / P&L)
Shows your revenue, expenses, and profit over a period (month, quarter, year).

```
Revenue                          $120,000
  - Cost of Goods Sold           - $30,000
= Gross Profit                    $90,000
  - Operating Expenses           - $45,000
= Net Profit                      $45,000
```

This is essentially your Schedule C in report form.

### Balance Sheet
Snapshot of what you own and owe at a specific point in time.

```
ASSETS                           LIABILITIES
  Cash            $25,000          Credit Card    $3,000
  Equipment       $15,000          Loan           $10,000
  Receivables      $8,000          Total         $13,000
  Total           $48,000
                                 EQUITY
                                   Owner's Equity $35,000
                                   Total         $35,000

Total Assets: $48,000 = Liabilities + Equity: $48,000
```

Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Always. If it doesn't balance, something is wrong.

### Cash Flow Statement
Shows where cash came from and where it went. Three sections:
1. **Operating activities** — cash from running the business
2. **Investing activities** — buying/selling equipment, property
3. **Financing activities** — loans, owner contributions/draws

Most small businesses focus on the P&L and reconcile the bank account. A formal cash flow statement is nice but not essential until you're growing.

## Recording Transactions

### Single Entry (Simple)
One line per transaction. Like a checkbook register:
- Date | Description | Income | Expense | Balance
- Good enough for very simple businesses
- Not suitable for tracking assets, liabilities, or equity

### Double Entry (Professional Standard)
Every transaction has two entries — a debit and a credit.
- Debit: left side (increases assets and expenses, decreases liabilities and income)
- Credit: right side (increases liabilities and income, decreases assets and expenses)

Example: You buy a $500 camera lens with your business credit card:
- Debit: Equipment Expense $500 (expense goes up)
- Credit: Credit Card Payable $500 (liability goes up)

Most accounting software (QuickBooks, FreshBooks, Wave) handles double entry behind the scenes. You just categorize transactions and it creates the journal entries.

## Owner's Draw vs. Salary

### Sole Proprietors (Schedule C)
You do NOT pay yourself a salary. You take an **owner's draw** — transfer money from business to personal account. This is NOT an expense and NOT deductible. It reduces your equity.

Your income is your net profit on Schedule C, regardless of how much you actually transferred to your personal account.

### S-Corp Owners
You MUST pay yourself a "reasonable salary" via W-2 payroll. Remaining profit can be distributed as non-wage distributions (not subject to SE tax). This is the primary tax advantage of S-Corp election.

## Accounting Software

### QuickBooks Online ($35-235/month)
Industry standard. Best for businesses that need invoicing, payroll, and reporting. Connects to 700+ bank/credit card feeds. Most CPAs prefer it.

### FreshBooks ($19-60/month)
Best for service businesses and freelancers. Excellent invoicing and time tracking. Simpler than QuickBooks.

### Wave (Free)
Free accounting and invoicing. Good enough for simple businesses. Paid add-ons for payroll and payments.

### Xero ($15-78/month)
Strong international support. Good for businesses with multiple currencies or locations.

### Spreadsheet
Excel or Google Sheets works for very simple tracking. Not recommended long-term — no bank feeds, no reconciliation, no audit trail, easy to make errors.

## Monthly Bookkeeping Checklist
1. Categorize all bank and credit card transactions
2. Record any cash transactions
3. Send outstanding invoices (if applicable)
4. Reconcile all bank accounts and credit cards
5. Review P&L — does it look right? Any unusual amounts?
6. Set aside estimated tax payment (25-30% of net profit)
7. Back up your data (or verify cloud sync)
