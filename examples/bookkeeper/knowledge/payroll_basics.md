# Payroll Basics for Small Business

## When You Need Payroll

### You Need Payroll If:
- You have **W-2 employees** (part-time or full-time)
- You're an **S-Corp owner** paying yourself a salary
- You have **statutory employees** (certain drivers, salespeople)

### You Don't Need Payroll If:
- You're a sole proprietor with no employees (take owner's draws)
- You only use independent contractors (issue 1099s instead)
- You're a single-member LLC without S-Corp election

## The Payroll Process

### 1. Before First Paycheck
- Get an EIN (irs.gov — free, 5 minutes)
- Register with your state for withholding and unemployment insurance
- Collect Form W-4 from each employee (determines federal withholding)
- Collect state withholding form (varies by state)
- Collect Form I-9 (employment eligibility verification)
- Set up payroll system or service

### 2. Each Pay Period
Calculate and withhold from employee's gross pay:
- **Federal income tax** (based on W-4 and IRS withholding tables)
- **Social Security tax** (employee share): 6.2% of wages up to $168,600 (2024)
- **Medicare tax** (employee share): 1.45% of all wages + 0.9% additional on wages over $200,000
- **State income tax** (varies)
- **State disability insurance** (some states: CA, NJ, NY, HI, RI)
- **Local taxes** (some cities/counties)

### 3. Employer's Share (You Pay This ON TOP of Wages)
- **Social Security tax** (employer share): 6.2% of wages up to $168,600
- **Medicare tax** (employer share): 1.45% of all wages
- **FUTA** (Federal Unemployment Tax): 6.0% on first $7,000 of each employee's wages, reduced to 0.6% with full state unemployment credit
- **SUTA** (State Unemployment Tax): varies by state and your experience rating (0.5-5%+ on first $7,000-$56,000 depending on state)
- **Workers' compensation insurance** (required in most states, based on industry risk class)

### Total Employer Cost
Budget **an additional 10-15% on top of wages** for employer payroll taxes and insurance.

Example: $50,000 salary
- Employer FICA: $3,825 (7.65%)
- FUTA: $42 (0.6% on first $7,000)
- SUTA: ~$200-500 (varies)
- Workers' comp: $200-2,000+ (varies by industry)
- **Total employer cost: ~$54,000-56,000**

## Tax Deposits (When to Pay the IRS)

### Federal Payroll Tax Deposits (Form 941 taxes)
Deposit schedule based on your total tax liability in the lookback period:
- **Monthly depositor** (tax liability under $50,000 in lookback period): Deposit by the 15th of the following month
- **Semi-weekly depositor** ($50,000+ in lookback period): Deposit within 3 business days of payday
- **Next-day deposit** required if you accumulate $100,000+ in any deposit period

### FUTA Deposits (Form 940)
- Deposit quarterly if liability exceeds $500
- Due by last day of month following the quarter end
- Annual filing on Form 940 due January 31

### State Tax Deposits
Vary by state. Most follow quarterly or monthly schedules similar to federal.

## Required Filings

| Form | What It Is | Due Date |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Form 941 | Quarterly federal payroll tax return | 4/30, 7/31, 10/31, 1/31 |
| Form 940 | Annual federal unemployment tax return | January 31 |
| Form W-2 | Annual wage statement (to employees) | January 31 |
| Form W-3 | Summary of all W-2s (to SSA) | January 31 |
| State quarterly reports | State unemployment + withholding | Varies by state |

## Payroll Services

### Full-Service (Recommended)
- **Gusto** ($40/mo + $6/employee) — Best for small businesses. Handles everything: calculations, filings, deposits, W-2s, 1099s. Employee self-service portal.
- **ADP Run** ($79/mo + $4/employee) — Industry standard. More features, higher cost.
- **Paychex Flex** (custom pricing) — Good for businesses with complex needs.
- **Square Payroll** ($35/mo + $6/employee) — Good if you use Square for payments.

### DIY (Not Recommended)
- Calculate withholding manually using IRS Publication 15
- Make deposits via EFTPS
- File forms yourself
- High risk of errors, penalties, and missed deadlines

### Your CPA/Bookkeeper
Many CPAs and bookkeeping firms offer payroll as a service. Often the best option if you already have a bookkeeper — they know your business.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Late deposits** — penalties range from 2% (1-5 days late) to 15% (10+ days late). The IRS does NOT forgive payroll tax penalties easily.
2. **Misclassifying employees as contractors** — see 1099 Rules module
3. **Not withholding enough** — if an employee's W-4 is wrong, they get a surprise tax bill and blame you
4. **Forgetting state requirements** — each state has different rules, rates, and deadlines
5. **Not keeping records** — retain payroll records for at least 4 years (6 years recommended)
6. **Paying family members without proper documentation** — yes, you can hire your kids (great tax strategy), but they need real W-4s, real paychecks, and real work performed

## Hiring Your Kids (Tax Strategy)

If you're a sole proprietor (not an S-Corp or partnership with non-parent partner):
- Children under 18: Exempt from FICA and FUTA
- Children under 21: Exempt from FUTA
- Each child gets their own standard deduction ($14,600 in 2024)
- Pay them for real work at a reasonable rate
- Moves income from your high bracket to their 0% bracket
- Up to $14,600 per child per year can be tax-free (their standard deduction)

**Requirements:** Real work, real pay, real records. Keep time sheets. Pay by check (not cash). The work must be legitimate and the pay must be reasonable for the work performed.
