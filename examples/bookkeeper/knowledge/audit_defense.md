# IRS Audit Risk and Defense

## Audit Rates (Reality Check)
The IRS audits about 0.4% of all individual returns. But rates vary dramatically:
- Income under $25,000: ~0.4% (mostly EITC verification)
- Income $25,000-$200,000: ~0.2% (lowest risk)
- Income $200,000-$1,000,000: ~0.5%
- Income over $1,000,000: ~1.1%
- Schedule C filers with losses: ~1.5-2%

The IRS uses a scoring system called **DIF (Discriminant Information Function)** that flags returns with unusual deduction-to-income ratios compared to similar taxpayers.

## Top Audit Triggers for Small Business

### 1. Reporting a Net Loss (Especially Multiple Years)
The IRS's hobby loss rule (Section 183) presumes a business without profit motive is a hobby. If you report losses for 3 of 5 consecutive years, the IRS may presume it's a hobby and disallow all deductions.

**Defense:** Document your profit motive — business plan, marketing efforts, time invested, expertise, expectation of future profit. Some legitimate businesses lose money for years before becoming profitable.

### 2. Cash-Intensive Business
Businesses that handle lots of cash (restaurants, retail, contractors) are higher risk because the IRS can't verify income from 1099s and W-2s alone.

**Defense:** Deposit ALL cash. Keep detailed daily records. Use a POS system with audit trails.

### 3. Unusually High Deductions
If your deductions are disproportionately high compared to your income (e.g., $80,000 in expenses on $50,000 revenue), expect scrutiny.

**Defense:** Every deduction must be documented with receipts and business purpose. If your ratio is legitimately high (startup year, major equipment purchase), be prepared to explain.

### 4. Home Office Deduction
The IRS knows this deduction is commonly abused. A $3,000 home office deduction on a $40,000 income return is normal. A $15,000 deduction on a $20,000 income return will get noticed.

**Defense:** Use the simplified method ($5/sq ft) for simplicity. If using actual, take photos of the dedicated space, keep utility bills, and ensure the space is EXCLUSIVELY used for business.

### 5. Vehicle Deduction Claiming 100% Business Use
The IRS is skeptical that any vehicle is used 100% for business unless it's a work truck that never goes home.

**Defense:** Keep a mileage log. Be honest about personal use. Even 90% business use is fine — claiming 100% when it's not true is a red flag.

### 6. Round Numbers
$10,000 in advertising. $5,000 in travel. $3,000 in meals. All round numbers signal estimation, not actual tracking.

**Defense:** Use actual amounts from receipts and bank statements. $9,847.32 in advertising looks real. $10,000 looks made up.

### 7. Large Charitable Contributions
Charitable deductions above 3% of AGI attract attention. Noncash contributions over $5,000 require a qualified appraisal.

### 8. Missing 1099 Income
The IRS matches every 1099 to your return. If a 1099-NEC shows $15,000 and your Schedule C shows $80,000 gross receipts, but your total 1099s add up to $95,000 — you've underreported.

**Defense:** Report ALL income, even amounts below the $600 1099 threshold. If a 1099 is incorrect, contact the issuer for a corrected form.

## Types of Audits

### Correspondence Audit (Most Common)
- IRS sends a letter asking for documentation of specific items
- You mail back copies of receipts, statements, or explanations
- Never send originals
- Most are resolved within 3-6 months

### Office Audit
- You (or your representative) visit the local IRS office
- Examiner reviews specific items in person
- Bring only what's requested — don't volunteer extra information

### Field Audit (Most Intensive)
- IRS agent visits your home or business
- Reviews books, records, and operations
- Can expand to additional years or issues
- This is serious — have a CPA or enrolled agent represent you

## Your Rights (Taxpayer Bill of Rights)

1. **Right to be informed** — know what's being examined and why
2. **Right to quality service** — prompt, professional, courteous
3. **Right to pay no more than correct amount**
4. **Right to challenge the IRS and be heard** — disagree with findings, provide evidence, appeal
5. **Right to appeal** — Independent Office of Appeals
6. **Right to finality** — know the maximum time for audit, when it's over
7. **Right to privacy** — IRS inquiries must be no more intrusive than necessary
8. **Right to confidentiality** — your information is protected
9. **Right to retain representation** — CPA, enrolled agent, or attorney can represent you
10. **Right to a fair tax system** — consideration of facts and circumstances

## Statute of Limitations

| Situation | IRS Must Audit Within |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Standard | 3 years from filing date |
| Underreported income by >25% | 6 years |
| Fraud | No limit |
| Didn't file at all | No limit |
| Filed amended return | 3 years from amended filing date (if extends assessment) |

If you filed on time (April 15), the clock starts April 15. If you filed late (September 1), the clock starts September 1.

**Keep records for 7 years to be safe** (covers the 6-year substantial understatement period plus buffer).

## How to Respond to an Audit Notice

1. **Don't panic.** Most audits are correspondence audits about specific items.
2. **Read the notice carefully.** It tells you exactly what's being examined.
3. **Don't ignore it.** Response deadlines are real. Missing them means the IRS decides without your input.
4. **Gather documentation** for the specific items in question. Receipts, bank statements, mileage logs, contracts.
5. **Consider representation.** A CPA or enrolled agent knows the process and can negotiate on your behalf.
6. **Respond only to what's asked.** Don't volunteer information about other years or other items.
7. **Send copies, not originals.** Always keep your originals.
8. **Document everything.** Send responses via certified mail or fax with confirmation.

## If You Disagree with the Findings

1. **Request a meeting with the examiner's supervisor**
2. **File a formal protest** with the IRS Independent Office of Appeals (free, no court required)
3. **Go to Tax Court** — file a petition within 90 days of the Notice of Deficiency (can represent yourself for amounts under $50,000 via Small Tax Case procedures)
4. **Pay and claim refund** — pay the assessment, then file a refund claim. If denied, sue in U.S. District Court or Court of Federal Claims.

## Prevention: Building an Audit-Proof Business

1. **Separate business and personal finances completely** — separate bank account, separate credit card
2. **Keep every receipt** — photograph with your phone, store digitally
3. **Use accounting software** — QuickBooks, FreshBooks, Wave. Paper systems invite errors.
4. **Reconcile monthly** — don't let transactions pile up
5. **Maintain a mileage log** — if you deduct vehicle expenses
6. **Document meals** — who, where, when, business purpose
7. **File all 1099s** — collect W-9s, track payments, file on time
8. **Report all income** — even cash, even amounts below $600
9. **Don't take deductions you can't prove** — if you lost the receipt and can't reconstruct the expense, don't deduct it
10. **File on time** — late filing draws attention and extends the statute of limitations
