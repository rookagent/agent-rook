# Lighting for Photographers

## Natural Light
- **Window light:** The most flattering portrait light. Subject faces the window, 45 degrees off for dimension. Sheer curtains = free softbox.
- **Open shade:** Under a tree, building overhang, or doorway. Even, soft light. Watch for color cast from green foliage overhead.
- **Golden hour:** 30-60 min before sunset. Warm, directional, magical. Backlight subjects for rim light and shoot into it with lens flare.
- **Overcast:** Giant softbox. Great for portraits (no squinting), tricky for landscapes (flat sky). Expose for the subject, let sky blow out or crop it.
- **Backlighting:** Subject between you and the light source. Expose for skin, accept blown highlights behind. Creates hair light and dreamy feel.

## Flash Fundamentals
- **Bounce flash:** Point the flash head at the ceiling or wall behind you. Creates soft, diffused light. Only works with light-colored surfaces.
- **Off-camera flash (OCF):** One light on a stand, 45 degrees to subject, with a modifier (softbox, umbrella). The single-light setup that covers 80% of situations.
- **Two-light setup:** Key light + rim/hair light from behind. Separates subject from background.
- **Drag the shutter:** Slow shutter (1/30–1/60) with rear-curtain flash. Captures ambient + freezes subject. Great for dance floors.
- **Reception lighting:** Bounce off ceiling when possible. If ceilings are dark/high, use a modifier on-camera (MagMod sphere) or plant OCF units around the room.
- **Gel your flash:** Match tungsten (CTO gel) or warm it slightly for skin. Daylight flash in tungsten rooms = blue/orange color war.

## Studio Patterns
- **Butterfly/Paramount:** Light directly above and in front. Creates shadow under nose. Classic beauty light.
- **Rembrandt:** 45-degree angle, creates triangle of light on shadow cheek. Classic portrait light with dimension.
- **Split:** Light from 90 degrees. Dramatic, moody. Half the face lit, half in shadow.
- **Clamshell:** Two lights — one above, one below (or reflector below). Eliminates shadows. Great for headshots.

## Problem Solving
- **Harsh midday sun:** Find open shade. Use a building as a flag. Or embrace it — hard shadows can be editorial.
- **Mixed lighting at receptions:** Don't fight DJ lights. Gel your flash to match the dominant ambient, or go full flash (overpower ambient at 1/200, f/5.6).
- **Dark venues:** Raise ISO, open up, plant OCF. Don't rely on bounce flash in a room with 30-foot dark ceilings.

## Exposure Tips
- **Expose to the right (ETTR):** In digital, slightly overexpose (without clipping highlights). More data in the highlights = cleaner shadows when you pull them back.
- **Histogram:** Use it. The LCD lies. Check for clipped highlights on skin.
- **Gray card:** Shoot one frame at each location change for easy white balance correction in post.
