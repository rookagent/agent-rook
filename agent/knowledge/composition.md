# Composition Fundamentals

## Rule of Thirds
Place key subjects at intersection points. In portraits, eyes go on the upper third line. In environmental shots, horizon on lower or upper third — never dead center unless symmetry is intentional.

## Leading Lines
Roads, railings, aisles, veils, arms — anything that draws the eye toward your subject. Diagonal lines add energy. Curved lines feel natural and romantic.

## Framing
Doorways, arches, windows, tree branches, even bridesmaids' arms during getting ready. Creates depth and draws focus inward.

## Negative Space
Don't fill every frame. Empty space (sky, wall, field) creates mood and breathing room. Great for album spreads that need text overlay space.

## Depth & Layering
Foreground + subject + background. Shoot through elements (foliage, candles, fabric) to add layers. Use wide apertures (f/1.4–f/2.8) to separate layers with bokeh.

## Group Composition
- **Triangles:** Odd numbers look better. Stagger heights.
- **Large groups (12+):** Use terrain (stairs, slopes) for rows. Largest group first, then dismiss.
- **Couples:** V-shape, closed embrace, walking, forehead touch — have 5 base poses and rotate.

## Detail Shots
Flat lays: shoot from directly above, arrange with spacing and symmetry. Rings, invitations, shoes, florals, perfume bottles. Use a consistent surface and natural light.

## When to Break the Rules
Center framing works for symmetrical venues (churches, grand hallways). Dutch angles work for editorial fashion, not family formals. Shoot tight on emotion — sometimes a face filling the entire frame is the strongest composition.
